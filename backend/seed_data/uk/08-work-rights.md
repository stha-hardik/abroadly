# Work Rights on a UK Student Visa

A Student visa allows part-time work during term and full-time work during vacations, subject to clear limits. Working beyond the limit is a serious immigration breach that can lead to deportation and a future-visa ban.

## The headline rules

[Source: GOV.UK Student visa work rules — https://www.gov.uk/student-visa/work]

- **During term-time:** **20 hours per week** maximum if you're on a course at **degree level (RQF 6 or above)** at a Higher Education Provider with Track Record sponsor status.
- **During term-time:** **10 hours per week** if you're studying below degree level.
- **During vacations:** Full-time, no hour cap.
- **Allowed** to be self-employed only via the Doctorate Extension Scheme — generally **not allowed** for a regular student to be self-employed or freelance.
- **Allowed** to work as a Student Union sabbatical officer (paid role) for up to 2 years.
- **Not allowed** to be a professional sportsperson, professional entertainer, or work as a doctor / dentist in training unless on the relevant programme.
- **Not allowed** to take a permanent full-time role at any time.

> **GAP:** The 20-hours-per-week cap was widely discussed for a one-time review by the Migration Advisory Committee — always check https://www.gov.uk/student-visa/work for the **current** number before relying on it.

## "Hours per week" — how the count works

- The week runs **Monday–Sunday**.
- It is **all paid work combined** — multiple part-time jobs count together.
- "Hours worked" means **scheduled or actually worked** — paid leave is not work, but a paid lunch break is.
- Going over even by 30 minutes in one week is a breach. UKVI takes this seriously.

## What counts as "term-time" vs "vacation"

Your university defines this in your enrolment letter. Roughly:
- **Term-time:** academic teaching weeks listed on your university's term-dates page.
- **Vacation:** official breaks (Christmas, Easter, summer), reading weeks usually still count as term-time unless your university says otherwise.
- **After your course ends** but before your Student visa expires, you can work full-time until the visa expiry date.

If you're unsure, ask your university's International Office to confirm the current term/vacation status in writing — keep the email; employers may ask.

## Typical part-time jobs

- **On-campus jobs:** library assistant, IT helpdesk, peer mentor, residence assistant, gym desk, café — usually £11–£13/hour. Easy to balance with study, employers understand visa restrictions.
- **Retail and hospitality:** Tesco, Sainsbury's, Costa, Pret, Wagamama, JD Wetherspoon — £11–£13/hour. Often 4–8 hour shifts; flexible scheduling but demanding during peak hours.
- **Delivery and gig:** Uber Eats, Deliveroo, Just Eat — **be careful**; some couriers are classed as self-employed which is **not allowed** on a Student visa. Verify your specific employment classification before signing up.
- **Tutoring:** maths, IELTS, programming — strong rates for those with the right skill. Usually paid through a tutoring agency (PAYE).
- **Internships and placements:** **paid placements as part of your course** can exceed the 20-hour cap if they're a credit-bearing part of the degree. Confirm with your university's Career Service in writing.

The UK minimum wage (April 2025 onwards) is around £12.21 per hour for age 21+ [Source: GOV.UK National Minimum Wage — https://www.gov.uk/national-minimum-wage-rates]. Most student jobs pay between minimum wage and £14/hour.

## Tax and National Insurance

- You **must** have a National Insurance number to work legally. Apply for one after you arrive at https://www.gov.uk/apply-national-insurance-number.
- All UK earnings are subject to income tax — but the **Personal Allowance is £12,570/year (2025/26)** [Source: HMRC — https://www.gov.uk/income-tax-rates], so most student earnings fall below the tax threshold.
- Employers deduct National Insurance contributions automatically.
- File a tax refund claim at year-end if too much tax was deducted — common for students who only work part of the year.

## Internships and placement years

Many UK degrees offer a **sandwich placement year** in industry (year 3 of a 4-year degree). On a Student visa this is allowed if:
- The placement is **assessed and credit-bearing** as part of your degree.
- It is **less than half** of the total course length.
- Your CAS reflects the placement structure.

If approved, you can work full-time for the placement year — and many students earn £18,000–£28,000 doing this. It often leads to a Graduate Route job offer with the same employer.

## What gets students in trouble

1. **Working over 20 hours in a single week during term.** UKVI can audit employer records.
2. **Cash-in-hand work** with no contract — easily flagged in any later visa application.
3. **Multiple jobs without tracking total hours.** Your tax record shows all of them.
4. **Working through a "self-employed" Uber Eats / Deliveroo account** — usually breaches the no-self-employment rule.
5. **Taking a full-time job while officially enrolled.** Even if your course load is light, you're enrolled full-time.

UKVI shares records with HMRC. Any visa application after a Student visa (Graduate Route, Skilled Worker) will be checked against your employment record. A breach now can permanently affect later visas.

## Working between courses

If you finish a bachelor's and are starting a master's, your Student visa permits **part-time work continuously** subject to the same rules. Confirm dates with your university — there's a brief gap between course end and master's start when full-time may or may not apply depending on your visa end date.

## Volunteering

Unpaid **volunteering** (true volunteering, not unpaid work for a commercial business) does **not** count against the 20-hour cap [Source: GOV.UK Student visa work rules — https://www.gov.uk/student-visa/work]. Good way to build CV without breaching visa.
